The Culture and Soundscape of Monglian/Tuvan Overtone Music

Overtone singing or throat singing is a technique that utilizes more than one note, which are sung simultaneously. Using this technique, singers can produce up to around three different notes which is unlike most other Western Music, or even most world music (Barras 1). While there are a handful of cultures that practice overtone singing, many of the overtone singers are concentrated in the Tuva, Altai, or Mongolia region (Levin 82).

Tuva is part of Russia as an autonomous republic in Siberia and borders Mongolia to the south. The Altai region is also in Siberia and Russia and also borders Mongolia to the south and Tuva to the east. Finally, Mongolia is a country north of China and south of Russia. While all three regions have their own distinct styles of throat singing and differences in culture, the throat singing styles are similar due to the cultural exchange and similarities in their culture. Throat singing was also first used by the nomadic herders in Mongolia/Siberia, which meant the singers would move around and not just stay in one place, further spreading their culture and music. According to √ñberg, the cultures are also similar since ‚Äúnomads do not really care about political nation-state borders; they see the landscape as the place where their herds can roam, borders for them look more like mountains and rivers‚Äù (16). Furthermore, all three regions share similar natural features and barriers like a desert, mountains, and rivers. The role of nature and land features in throat singing become even more prevalent to the culture since legends say throat singing is originally meant to imitate the sounds of nature -- the sounds of the wind and the sounds of the rivers (Levin 80).

Many of the cultures that practice overtone singing often believe that overtone singing was ‚Äúlinked with powerful forces within the Universe,‚Äù so the music is used for meditation or even healing (Pegg 32). Some even believed that the overtone sounds were harmful to the body (Pegg 31). Throat singing is very much linked to nature since many believe that throat singing imitates birds, wind as it goes through mountains, or rivers (Pegg 38). Tuvan throat singing is especially connected to nature due to Tuvan beliefs where ‚Äúnatural objects and phenomena have souls or are inhabited by spirits‚Äù (Levin 80). This belief is called animalism and is not unique to the Tuvans. According to these beliefs, sounds also are a part of what makes up the ‚Äúsoul‚Äù of a mountain or other part of nature, and by imitating these sounds, Tuvans herders believe they can ‚Äúassimilate this power‚Äù (Levin 80).

Throat singing was initially very much an outside activity since most singers were herders or hunters (√ñberg 1). One setting throat singing is used in is herding animals like yaks and camels. Some Mongolian herdsmen claimed that they had once heard singing that could be heard from as far as 3 kilometers (Pegg 36). The throat singing is also used for celebrations like weddings where singers perform with dancers and fiddlers. Today, throat singing is no longer solely practiced by isolated nomadic herders; there are now many regional and international competitions for throat singing, internationally recognized throat-singing bands and groups, and non-native learners of throat singing (√ñberg 1).

Overtone singing is truly unique in that it is only performed by certain cultures, and thus many Western audiences have never heard such music.
